Am I Connected?

Separate self, or joined to all — the unity beneath every being

सर्वं खल्विदं ब्रह्म
Sarvam Khalvidam Brahma — All This Is Verily Brahman

Am I a separate self, sealed inside my own skin — or joined, at some deep level, to everything else? The Upanishads gave the most radical answer: there is no final separation at all. Sarvam khalvidam brahma — all this is verily Brahman (Chandogya Upanishad 3.14.1); and Tat tvam asi — That thou art (Chandogya 6.8.7). The boundary you feel between yourself and the world is real enough to live by, but it is not the deepest truth of what you are. The one who sees all beings in the Self, and the Self in all beings, shrinks from nothing (Isha Upanishad 6). Each tradition below traces that connection in its own way — and from it, almost without exception, draws the same conclusion: care.

How 12 Traditions See Our Connection

VEDIC TRADITION
तत्त्वमसि (Tat Tvam Asi — Thou Art That)

"Tat tvam asi" — That thou art (Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7). The deepest connection is identity: the Self in you (Atman) is the one Self in all (Brahman). "He who sees all beings in his own Self, and his Self in all beings, hates none" (Isha Upanishad 6). To injure another is, finally, to injure oneself; compassion is not a commandment but a recognition.

ISLAMIC TRADITION
أمة (Ummah — One Community)

"Hold firmly to the rope of God, all together, and do not be divided" (Qur'an 3:103). Humanity is one ummah, called to unity; and the bond reaches past humankind — "there is no creature on earth nor bird that flies but they are communities like you" (Qur'an 6:38). All things glorify their Maker (Qur'an 17:44): one creation, turned toward one Source.

CHRISTIAN TRADITION
The Body of Christ

"We, though many, are one body in Christ, and individually members one of another" (Romans 12:5). The image is an organism: when one part suffers, all suffer. On this hangs the second commandment — "love your neighbour as yourself" (Mark 12:31) — and the startling claim that what is done to the least is done to Christ himself (Matthew 25:40).

BUDDHIST TRADITION
Pratītyasamutpāda (Dependent Arising)

Nothing stands alone: all things arise in mutual dependence (pratītyasamutpāda) — this is, because that is. The Avatamsaka Sutra pictures Indra's Net, an infinite web in which every jewel reflects all the others. From this seamless interdependence flows karuna, compassion: when the line between self and other thins, the suffering of another is no longer foreign.

SIKH TRADITION
ਜੋਤਿ (Jot — One Divine Light)

"From the One Light, the entire universe welled up" (Guru Granth Sahib). The same divine spark shines in every being, so the Sikh prays not for the self but for sarbat da bhala — the welfare of all — and serves through langar and seva, the free kitchen where every rank sits in one row. To see the One in all is to serve all as One.

JEWISH TRADITION
צֶלֶם אֱלֹהִים (Tzelem Elohim — Image of God)

The Mishnah teaches that humanity was created from a single person so that no one could say "my lineage is greater than yours" (Sanhedrin 4:5) — and that to save one life is to save a world. All bear the image of God; "love your neighbour as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18); and we are charged with tikkun olam, the repair of a shared world.

TAOIST TRADITION
齊物 (Qí Wù — All Things as One)

"Heaven and earth were born together with me, and the ten thousand things and I are one" (Zhuangzi). The same Tao flows through every being; yin and yang arise only together. To grasp this is to drop the anxious self that sets itself against the world, and to move with the whole — sensing one's kinship with the river, the mountain, the turning season.

BAHA'I TRADITION
Oneness of Humanity

"The earth is but one country, and mankind its citizens" (Bahá'u'lláh). The oneness of humanity is the pivot of the Bahá'í teachings — not a distant ideal but the next stage of our common life. "Ye are the fruits of one tree, and the leaves of one branch": the diversity of peoples is the variation of a single family, made to know and cherish one another.

ZOROASTRIAN TRADITION
Stewardship of Creation

For Zoroastrians, the world is Ahura Mazda's good creation, and human beings are its caretakers, charged to protect the seven creations — sky, water, earth, plants, animals, fire and humankind. To foul a river or wrong a creature is to damage the shared fabric of the good world. Connection here is responsibility: every good thought, word and deed strengthens the whole.

JAIN TRADITION
परस्परोपग्रहो जीवानाम् (Parasparopagraho Jivanam)

"Parasparopagraho jivanam" — all life is bound together by mutual support (Tattvartha Sutra 5.21), the very motto on the emblem of Jainism. No soul is an island: each depends on countless others. From this follows ahimsa — harm none — not as restraint alone but as the lived knowledge that every living thing, however small, shares one web with you.

SUFI TRADITION
بنی آدم (Bani Adam — Children of Adam)

The Sufi sees the one Beloved gazing back from every face. Saadi's lines, inscribed at the United Nations, put it plainly: "The children of Adam are limbs of one body, created from a single essence; if one limb is pained, the others cannot rest." All beings are waves of one ocean; to love God is to love what God has made.

CONFUCIAN TRADITION
萬物一體 (One Body with All Things)

"Within the four seas, all men are brothers" (Analects 12.5). For Confucius the human being exists only in relationship — the self is a knot in a web of bonds, and ren, co-humanity, is the thread. The Neo-Confucian Wang Yangming went furthest: the truly humane person "forms one body with Heaven, Earth, and the myriad things," feeling another's suffering as a pain in his own flesh.

The Illusion of Separateness

Of all the questions in this collection, this is where the traditions draw closest. Press almost any of them to its depth and the same answer returns: the sense of being a walled-off, separate self is not the final truth. The Upanishads say it most directly — Tat Tvam Asi, "Thou art That" — you and the ground of all being are not two. But the Buddhist sees it in dependent arising, the Christian in one body, the Sufi and Taoist in the single ocean of which all are waves, the Bahá'í in one tree and its many leaves, the Jain in the mutual support that binds every life. The boundary we feel between self and world is real enough to live by, and not the last word about what we are.

And from this, ethics follows — not as a rule imposed from outside but as the natural shape of seeing clearly. If the other is, at the deepest level, not other, then compassion is simply accuracy. Love your neighbour as yourself; ahimsa, harming none; karuna, the compassion that flows when separateness dissolves; sarbat da bhala, the welfare of all. Here the convergence is striking. Yet they do not ground it identically: for the Vedantin all beings are literally one Self; for the Buddhist there is no self at all, only an unbroken web of mutual arising; for the theist we are distinct creatures bound in kinship under one God. That disagreement is real and worth keeping. But across every one of them runs a single, practical conclusion: you are not as separate as you feel — so live, and love, accordingly.
